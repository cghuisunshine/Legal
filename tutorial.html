Module 0 ‚Äî Ethical Thinking Toolkit foundation
A repeatable method for analyzing any IT ethics dilemma.
‚ñ∂

Core idea: Ethics isn‚Äôt ‚Äúvibes.‚Äù It‚Äôs a structured way to reason about choices that affect people.

Step-by-step Ethical Decision Framework

  1. Describe the technology and the decision point.
  2. List stakeholders (users, company, public, vulnerable groups).
  3. Identify values/rights involved (privacy, safety, freedom, fairness).
  4. Test actions with ethical theories (below).
  5. Consider consequences (short and long term).
  6. Choose + justify (clear reasoning, not slogans).

Ethical Theories (Quick Guide)

  • Kantian: Are people treated as ends, not tools?
  • Act utilitarian: In this case, do benefits outweigh harms?
  • Rule utilitarian: If everyone followed this rule, would it be good?
  • Social contract: Would society reasonably accept this norm?
  • Virtue ethics: What character/professional habits does this build?

How to write a strong ethics answer

Name the stakeholders ‚Üí name the harm/benefit ‚Üí apply at least two theories ‚Üí conclude with a justified decision and tradeoffs.

Study & Reflection Prompt

Pick a real tech you used today (maps, email, social media, cloud storage). What is the ethical ‚Äúdecision point‚Äù hidden inside it? (Example: ‚ÄúShould location history be on by default?‚Äù)

Module 1 ‚Äî Catalysts for Change technology ‚Üî society
Why the Information Age happened, and why ethics becomes unavoidable.
‚ñ∂

Core idea: The Information Age is driven by low-cost computers and high-speed networks. These solve problems‚Äîbut also create new ones.

Key Concepts

  • Technology adoption is a choice, not destiny.
  • ‚ÄúDo we drive technology, or does technology drive us?‚Äù
  • New tools reshape habits, relationships, and power.

Practical Example

Smartphones feel empowering (navigation, payments, safety). But they also create constant data trails and ‚Äúalways-on‚Äù expectations.

Ethical frameworks applied

Utilitarian: Net benefits vs harms to society.
Virtue ethics: Does reliance on tech build good habits (prudence, empathy), or weaken them?

Study & Reflection Prompt

Explanation note: Module 1 trains you to see ‚Äúinnovation‚Äù and ‚Äúethics‚Äù as linked, not separate.

Module 2 ‚Äî Networked Communication spam ¬∑ speech ¬∑ censorship
The Internet amplifies communication‚Äîboth helpful and harmful.
‚ñ∂

Core idea: Networked communication improves coordination and access‚Äîbut also enables misuse (spam, deception, censorship conflicts, addiction).

Spam as an ethical case

  • Spam is cheap for senders, expensive for everyone else (time, attention, infrastructure).
  • It exploits design gaps: early email wasn‚Äôt built with social norms in mind.

Freedom of expression (not absolute)

  • Speech rights must be balanced against public good.
  • Online scale + anonymity makes regulation difficult and controversial.

Ethical frameworks applied (Spam example)

Kantian: Treats recipients as tools (attention extraction).
Rule utilitarian: If everyone used inboxes for unsolicited campaigns, communication collapses.
Social contract: Reasonable people would reject ‚Äúinbox pollution‚Äù as a norm.

Study & Reflection Prompt

Choose one: spam, censorship, child safety filters, or ‚Äúinternet addiction.‚Äù Write a 6‚Äì8 sentence analysis using two ethical theories.

Explanation note: Module 2 shows how ‚Äútechnical design‚Äù and ‚Äúsocial expectations‚Äù must be solved together.

Module 3 ‚Äî Intellectual Property copyright ¬∑ fair use ¬∑ DRM
Digital copying is easy‚Äîso what counts as ‚Äústealing,‚Äù ‚Äúsharing,‚Äù or ‚Äúfair use‚Äù?
‚ñ∂

Core idea: Intellectual property (IP) is valuable, but unlike physical property it is infinitely copyable. That forces society to decide how much exclusivity is fair.

Key Concepts

  • IP = unique creations of the mind with commercial value (music, software, books).
  • Copying isn‚Äôt identical to taking a physical object (owner still has it).
  • Law tries to balance incentives for creators with access for society.

Fair Use (Why it matters)

Society benefits when teachers, researchers, and critics can quote/transform works in limited ways‚Äîwithout asking permission every time.

Ethical frameworks applied

Utilitarian: Does stronger IP protection increase creativity overall‚Äîor block remix/innovation?
Social contract: Would people accept a system that pays creators while still allowing education and commentary?

Study & Reflection Prompt

You want to use a copyrighted image in a class website. What are 3 safer alternatives (e.g., Creative Commons, public domain, your own)? Explain which choice best balances creator rights and learning value.

Explanation note: This module trains you to separate ‚Äúlegal rules‚Äù from ‚Äúethical justifications,‚Äù then connect them.

Module 4 ‚Äî Information Privacy data trails ¬∑ profiling
Privacy as control over access‚Äîplus the ethics of data mining and consent.
‚ñ∂

Core idea: Privacy is best understood as control over access to personal information and the self‚Äînot ‚Äúhaving secrets.‚Äù

Why privacy matters

  • Protects dignity and autonomy.
  • Supports healthy relationships and trust.
  • Reduces manipulation and discrimination risk.

Data trails + data mining

  • Daily life generates records (location, shopping, web browsing, health).
  • Data mining combines datasets to predict behavior and preferences.
  • This can enable targeted services‚Äîor unfair profiling.

Opt-in vs Opt-out (ethical comparison)

Opt-in: sharing happens only after clear consent (more respectful of autonomy).
Opt-out: sharing is default; user must fight the system (often hidden, confusing).

Study & Reflection Prompt

Pick one app you use. Identify: (1) what data it collects, (2) whether consent is opt-in or opt-out, (3) one change that would make it more ethical without killing usefulness.

Explanation note: Module 4 helps you argue privacy issues beyond ‚ÄúI feel uncomfortable‚Äù‚Äîyou can name rights, harms, and design choices.

Module 5 ‚Äî Privacy and Government security vs liberty
Surveillance, law, and the ongoing tension between safety and freedom.
‚ñ∂

Core idea: Governments claim surveillance for safety, but surveillance can also damage democratic freedoms and trust.

What governments collect

  • Census-style personal records
  • Financial and employment data
  • Crime databases and ‚Äúwatchlists‚Äù
  • Wiretaps and covert monitoring

Ethical risks

  • Innocent people swept into suspicion
  • Mission creep (new uses for old data)
  • Normalization of intrusion
  • Chilling effects on speech and dissent

Ethical frameworks applied

Social contract: What surveillance would citizens accept if rules were transparent and reciprocal?
Utilitarian: Are we actually safer, and is the cost to liberty justified?

Study & Reflection Prompt

Design a ‚Äúfair rule‚Äù for government data collection in emergencies. Include: scope, time limit, oversight, and appeal process.

Explanation note: Module 5 builds skill at balancing competing public goods (security) and individual rights (privacy).

Module 6 ‚Äî Computer & Network Security hacking ¬∑ malware ¬∑ phishing
Security failures are ethical failures when people are harmed by preventable risk.
‚ñ∂

Core idea: Security isn‚Äôt only technical‚Äîsystems protect real people (money, identity, safety).

Common threats

  • Unauthorized access (hacking)
  • Malware (worms, trojans, ransomware)
  • Phishing (deception for credentials)
  • DoS attacks (service disruption)

Practical defenses

  • Long unique passwords + MFA
  • Updates/patching
  • HTTPS and secure Wi-Fi habits
  • Least privilege + monitoring

Responsible disclosure idea

Tools that reveal vulnerabilities can be ethically justified when they reduce harm (by forcing fixes), but they raise questions about timing, intent, and preventable misuse.

Study & Reflection Prompt

Imagine a researcher finds a major vulnerability. Write a disclosure plan: who to notify, timeline, proof-of-concept limits, and public communication.

Explanation note: Module 6 makes ‚Äúsecurity‚Äù part of ethics: negligence becomes moral responsibility when harm is foreseeable.

Module 7 ‚Äî Computer Reliability bugs ‚Üí real harm
From billing mistakes to life-critical failures: reliability is a moral obligation.
‚ñ∂

Core idea: Systems fail in small ways (annoying) and catastrophic ways (fatal). Reliability depends on engineering discipline.

Failure types

  • Bad input or misinterpreted data
  • Software logic errors
  • Complex system interactions
  • Overreliance on software with no safeguards

Reliability principles

  • No single point of failure
  • Redundancy + audit trails
  • Verification (built correctly)
  • Validation (built the right thing)

Ethical frameworks applied

Utilitarian: Preventing rare catastrophic harm can outweigh cost savings.
Virtue ethics: Reliability culture reflects professional character: care, humility, rigor.

Study & Reflection Prompt

Write a checklist for a high-stakes system release (medical, transportation, finance). Include testing, monitoring, rollback plan, and accountability.

Explanation note: Module 7 connects engineering practices (testing, redundancy) to moral outcomes (harm prevention).

Module 8 ‚Äî Professional Ethics codes ¬∑ duty ¬∑ whistleblowing
Why computing professionals carry special responsibility‚Äîand how codes guide decisions.
‚ñ∂

Core idea: Technical work scales. When your systems affect thousands or millions, your ethical duty increases.

What makes a ‚Äúprofession‚Äù mature?

  • Education standards + accreditation
  • Certification and/or licensing

  • Code of ethics + enforcement culture

Software Engineering Code (themes)

  • Public interest comes first
  • Honesty in judgment and communication
  • Quality, safety, privacy, and fairness
  • Mentorship and responsibility to colleagues

Explanation note: Module 8 trains you to treat ‚Äúbeing a developer‚Äù like a role with duties‚Äînot only technical tasks.

Module 9 ‚Äî Work, Wealth & Automation jobs ¬∑ inequality ¬∑ digital divide
Automation and globalization reshape who benefits from technology‚Äîand who gets left behind.
‚ñ∂

Core idea: Technology can raise productivity yet still increase inequality. Benefits don‚Äôt distribute automatically.

Automation & employment

  • Destroys some jobs, creates others
  • Can increase output without reducing work hours
  • May concentrate rewards (‚Äúwinner-take-all‚Äù effects)

  • Not just device access

Ethical frameworks applied

Justice/fairness lens: Who bears the cost of progress?
Social contract: What obligations do tech-enabled winners owe to society (training, safety nets, access)?

Study & Reflection Prompt

Explanation note: Module 9 extends ethics beyond ‚Äúapps‚Äù to economic and social outcomes.
